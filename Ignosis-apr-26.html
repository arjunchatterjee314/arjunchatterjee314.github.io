Part 02

What Does the Company Do?

Company Description

Ignosis is a B2B fintech infrastructure company that provides banks, NBFCs, insurers, and wealth managers with the technology to access, interpret, and act on consented financial data in real time. The platform operates across two interconnected layers: an Account Aggregator infrastructure layer that fetches and orchestrates encrypted financial data from multiple AA providers on behalf of institutional customers, and an AI analytics layer that converts raw financial data into income verification scores, credit risk signals, fraud indicators, MSME cashflow profiles, collections intelligence, and hyper-personalised engagement nudges. Ignosis was incorporated in January 2022 and is headquartered in Ahmedabad, Gujarat.[1] In February 2026, the company launched end-to-end AI Collections, adding Voice AI agents and smart mandate optimisation to the product suite.[5]

One-Line Pitch

The intelligence layer between India's Account Aggregator rails and the credit decisions of 150-plus financial institutions, converting consented financial data into automated underwriting, personalised engagement, and AI-powered collections at scale.

2.1 The Problem

India's formal credit system operates on a structural paradox. More than 160 million Indian consumers remain credit-underserved despite the country processing over INR 166 lakh crore in UPI transactions annually.[4] The gap is not a demand problem. It is a data problem: specifically, the inability of financial institutions to verify income, assess repayment capacity, and price risk accurately for borrowers who fall outside the narrow band of salaried, formally employed individuals whose credit profiles are legible to legacy underwriting systems.

For the past decade, banks, NBFCs, and fintech lenders have relied on PDF bank statement analysis as the primary input for credit decisioning. The system works adequately for salaried employees with stable, recurring income patterns. It breaks down entirely for gig workers and the self-employed, MSME proprietors, and thin-file borrowers, three cohorts that together represent the majority of India's credit-eligible but credit-underserved population.[6] The PDF approach carries a second structural failure: it is fraudable by design. As Ignosis has documented in its own published research, the most commonly used PDF generation libraries are the same tools used both by banks to generate legitimate statements and by fraudsters to produce convincing fakes. The underwriting logic collapses at precisely the decision boundary it was built to handle.[6]

The Account Aggregator framework, launched by the RBI in September 2021, was designed to dissolve this problem at the infrastructure level. Under AA, a borrower grants explicit, time-limited, purpose-bound consent for their financial data to be fetched directly from source institutions in encrypted, standardised format, without a PDF intermediary. The data cannot be tampered with because it never passes through the borrower's hands. This eliminates the fraud vector and simultaneously makes the income and cashflow patterns of gig workers, MSME owners, and thin-file borrowers as legible to a credit model as those of salaried employees.[7] The problem is that most financial institutions cannot use this infrastructure effectively without significant technical investment. Connecting to multiple AA providers, orchestrating consent flows, normalising data from disparate source formats, and converting raw transaction feeds into credit-grade analytics is engineering work that most banks and NBFCs lack the internal capability to execute well. Three years after the framework went live, the bottleneck is not the infrastructure's existence. It is the absence of a turnkey layer that makes it actionable. That is the specific gap Ignosis occupies.

2.2 The Solution

AA Orchestration is the core infrastructure layer. Ignosis integrates with multiple AA providers simultaneously and automatically routes each consent request to the best-performing provider in real time. The company reports an end-to-end AA success rate above 85 percent and processes over 200,000 consented data pulls per day.[8] Integration is available via API with no upfront cost, and deployment timelines are measured in days.

Income Analysis and MSME Underwriting are the analytics modules built on top of the AA data feed. Income Analysis applies machine learning models to transaction history retrieved through AA to verify income, infer repayment capacity, and generate credit signals for salaried employees, gig workers, and self-employed individuals, including borrower profiles that produce no usable output from a PDF parser. MSME Underwriting extends this to business borrowers, profiling merchant turnover, cashflow volatility, Fixed Obligation to Income Ratio, and business risk from a combination of bank account data and GST filing history.

Customer Data Intelligence and Personal Finance Management convert financial behaviour signals embedded in AA data into propensity scores and contextual nudges for cross-sell and upsell, and a unified financial dashboard that lenders deploy to deepen customer relationships.

AI Collections, launched February 2026, adds three AI-driven tools: Case Allocation, which prioritises a lender's collections queue by predicting each borrower's ability and intent to repay; Smart Mandate, which identifies the optimal timing and amount for direct debit representations; and Voice AI agents that automate outbound collections conversations with contextual awareness of each borrower's financial situation.[5]

Partnership Lending connects the platform to ONDC-FS and OCEN, enabling lenders to access new distribution channels through pre-integrated digital lending journeys.

The platform is delivered via API, available cloud-hosted or on-premise for institutions with data residency requirements under the DPDP Act. Ignosis holds ISO 27001 and SOC 2 Type II certifications and is built on RBI-aligned compliance architecture.[8] All modules are generally available and live in production with more than 150 financial institutions as of early 2026. AI Collections is in active pilot.

The Data Flywheel

Ignosis's AI models are trained on the transaction data and consent outcomes flowing through the platform from its institutional customer base. Each consent request and its downstream outcome, whether approved, rejected, defaulted, or prepaid, becomes a labelled training signal that improves model accuracy for the next comparable borrower profile. The company has profiled over 50 million user financial profiles through this system.[8] That dataset, specifically its coverage of thin-file, gig, and MSME borrower cashflow patterns in the Indian market, is not replicable by a competitor entering today without rebuilding Ignosis's entire customer acquisition history from scratch. The flywheel is in motion. Whether the company is moving fast enough to build switching-cost depth before a better-capitalised competitor reaches the same product position is the central question this memo addresses.

Part 04

Market Opportunity

Ignosis is not a lender. It is a technology vendor that sells to banks seeking better underwriting, NBFCs and fintech lenders seeking faster credit decisioning, and insurance and wealth firms needing financial behaviour signals. Its addressable market is the technology and data intelligence spend of regulated financial institutions, not the lending or insurance markets themselves.

The India AI in BFSI market was valued at approximately INR 9,690 crore in 2024 and is projected to reach approximately INR 68,250 crore by 2033, growing at a compound annual growth rate of 28.8 percent.[21] Within India's fintech sector, BFSI SaaS companies represented approximately 45 percent of private fintech funding in 2024 by value, a structural shift toward institutions purchasing software rather than building internal capability.[23]

The AA ecosystem provides the most direct basis for sizing the near-term serviceable market. As of September 2025, the framework has 112 financial institutions live as both Financial Information Providers and Users, 410 live solely as FIUs, and over 2.2 billion financial accounts enabled for consent-based data sharing.[25] The ecosystem facilitated services for 24.4 million users in FY2025, with NBFCs accounting for 60 percent of total completed consents, and HDFC Bank's own executive team has described AA adoption as growing at 20 percent month-on-month.[26][27] At a directional baseline subscription of INR 10 lakh per institution per year for the orchestration layer alone, and with 570-plus regulated institutions now participating, the contracted revenue opportunity approaches INR 570 crore for a platform capturing a meaningful share, before the analytics and collections modules are priced in.

Ignosis's current confirmed customer base of 150-plus institutions implies, at INR 8 lakh to INR 12 lakh average annual contract value across the full platform, an annual recurring revenue run rate of INR 12 crore to INR 18 crore. This range is consistent with the MCA-reported FY2025 revenue of INR 10.6 crore, noting that the AI Collections module had not yet been commercially deployed in that period. Scaling to 300 active institutions with higher average contract values as AI modules reach general availability, the 24-month revenue ceiling from the domestic institutional base approaches INR 40 crore to INR 60 crore.

4.1 Market Dynamics

The AA ecosystem has crossed the adoption threshold that makes Ignosis's product operationally necessary rather than optionally interesting. In December 2023, AA had 39 million linked accounts. By December 2024, that figure had grown to 120 million. By September 2025, 2.2 billion financial accounts were enabled for consent-based sharing and USD 10 billion-plus in loans had been disbursed through the ecosystem, with half of that total disbursed in the six months from April to September 2024 alone.[25][30] An institution that delays AA integration today is not behind the curve. It is exiting the market for the highest-quality borrower cohorts. Ignosis is selling into urgency, not education.

The DPDP Act compliance clock is running and directly expands demand for AA-native data infrastructure. The Digital Personal Data Protection Rules 2025, notified in November 2025, impose full substantive compliance obligations from May 2027, with consent manager registration obligations from November 2026 and penalties up to INR 250 crore per contravention.[32] Financial institutions relying on PDF parsing, screen scraping, or bulk data collection to power credit decisioning now face a live compliance liability. Ignosis's AA-native architecture, in which all data is fetched through user-consented, encrypted, time-limited, purpose-specific consent requests, is structurally compliant by construction. The DPDP Act is not a headwind for Ignosis. It is a procurement accelerator. Sahamati has further argued that operating Account Aggregators should be deemed Consent Managers for the financial sector under the DPDP framework without requiring separate registration, which would convert Ignosis's institutional customers into DPDP-compliant data fiduciaries automatically.[43] If that regulatory outcome is confirmed, it shortens the compliance sales cycle at precisely the moment when enforcement urgency is highest.

The AI inflection in Indian BFSI has reached the specific product categories Ignosis launched into in early 2026. The first wave of BFSI AI adoption, covering chatbots and customer support automation, is now a competed market. The second wave, covering underwriting intelligence and collections automation, is beginning to generate the verifiable institutional outcomes that drive procurement decisions: Bajaj Finance reported INR 150 crore per year in savings from GenAI applications; Tata Capital reduced customer service turnaround from 24 hours to 20 minutes; mid-sized banks reported 34 to 36 percent reductions in credit disbursement and collection costs.[61] Ignosis's AI Collections module launched at precisely the moment when the market is beginning to pay for verified outcomes rather than AI demonstration capabilities. India's total system credit outstanding stands at INR 189 lakh crore as of September 2025.[28] Collections infrastructure for even a marginal improvement in recovery rates across this base represents enormous addressable spend.

Every expansion of the India Stack enlarges Ignosis's addressable customer base. Credit Line on UPI, extended to Small Finance Banks in December 2024, represents a new product category requiring AA-based income verification. GSTN is now a live Financial Information Provider in the AA ecosystem, enabling GST filing data to flow as a consent-based data source for MSME underwriting. The RBI is actively working to add CBDT and EPFO as Financial Information Providers.[34] Each regulatory addition to the AA ecosystem is simultaneously a product enrichment for Ignosis and a fresh reason for an institution not yet on the platform to initiate a procurement conversation.

Part 06

Competition and Differentiation

Ignosis's current advantage rests on three things: an AA-native architecture calibrated to how Indian financial institutions actually need to consume consented data, a production track record with named enterprise customers that no competitor at its funding level can match in the AA intelligence layer specifically, and a founder governance position inside the AA ecosystem that provides structural information advantages no later entrant can acquire quickly. That advantage is real and present. It is also bounded. The most heavily capitalised competitor in the adjacent space, Perfios, raised INR 1,920 crore in its Series D and is actively building AI capabilities on top of an existing financial data platform serving over 1,000 institutions. The capital disparity is a structural fact.

Direct Competitors

Perfios

Founded 2008  ·  Total funding: approximately INR 3,225 crore  ·  Revenue: INR 709 crore FY2025  ·  Valuation: INR 10,100 crore

Perfios is the most direct structural comparable and the most important competitive threat. It operates Anumati, a licensed NBFC-AA, and has recently launched a GenAI platform for banking (July 2025) and an AI operating system for BFSI (December 2025). It has 1,042 employees and 1,000-plus institutional customers across 18 countries.[39]

The competitive distinction is one of age, scope, and go-to-market model. Perfios built its dominant position over seventeen years primarily on document parsing rather than consent-based AA data. Its AA capability via Anumati is a more recent addition to a platform whose institutional relationships were established on a different data access model. Ignosis was built from inception on the AA framework, and its 85-plus percent end-to-end AA success rate reflects years of exclusive focus on this layer rather than an added feature. That said, Perfios has INR 3,225 crore in funding against Ignosis's INR 46 crore, 1,000-plus institutional customers against 150-plus, and is now actively investing in AI capabilities that directly overlap with the intelligence and collections modules Ignosis has built. Perfios can build toward Ignosis's product position on a deliberate engineering roadmap without requiring new customer acquisition.

FinBox

Founded 2015  ·  Total funding: approximately INR 4,285 crore  ·  130-plus institutional customers including HDFC Bank, Kotak Mahindra Bank, Tata Capital

FinBox describes itself as the operating system for digital lending and covers credit origination, loan origination software, bank statement analysis, alternate data underwriting, and multi-bureau analytics. Its BankConnect product includes AA integration as one of several data sources. In September 2025 it raised INR 3,360 crore led by WestBridge Capital with stated plans to build agentic AI workflows and a fraud intelligence suite.[40]

FinBox is more complementary than directly competing in primary use cases today: an NBFC looking for a loan origination system goes to FinBox; an NBFC looking for the best available AA integration and financial intelligence signals layer goes to Ignosis. The risk is product expansion. FinBox's stated roadmap includes agentic AI, and it has the Series B capital to extend its product scope in Ignosis's direction.

Differentiation: What Ignosis Genuinely Has

Three points of differentiation hold up under honest scrutiny. The first is AA-native architecture with the deepest multi-AA orchestration capability in the market. The L&T Finance case study documents above 80 percent AA conversion rates in a production environment processing 10,000-plus daily transactions. No other TSP in the market has published equivalent third-party validated conversion metrics at this transaction scale. This is a consequence of four years of exclusive focus on AA orchestration, not a feature replicable in a few engineering sprints.[36]

The second is the proprietary financial intelligence dataset. Ignosis has profiled over 50 million user financial profiles through its platform.[8] Every consent request and its downstream credit outcome becomes a labelled training signal. The dataset is India-specific, thin-file and gig-economy borrower profile-heavy, and accumulated over four years of live production deployments. Perfios has a larger and older dataset but one built primarily on document parsing. The AA-native signal set is qualitatively different, not a subset of what Perfios holds.

The third is the founder's governance position inside the AA ecosystem. Nirav Prajapati's role as Joint-Convenor of Sahamati's Associate Member TSP Steering Committee means Ignosis has direct visibility into the regulatory direction of the AA ecosystem before it becomes public, and participatory influence over the standards that all AA participants must follow.[14] This is structural information asymmetry that compounds over time.

Ignosis does not have a structural technology moat that a well-resourced competitor cannot eventually replicate. The moat it is building is a combination of data density, switching cost, and ecosystem positioning. The switching cost component is the institutional dependency created when a lender rebuilds its underwriting workflow around Ignosis's AA signals: the credit models are calibrated to Ignosis's specific income detection and cashflow categorisation outputs, and replacing that layer requires recalibrating the entire underwriting stack. Whether this combination compounds faster than Perfios's engineering investment in AA-native AI over the next 18 to 24 months is the central competitive question the Series A must answer. The race is active.

Part 07

Business Model and Unit Economics

Ignosis's pricing architecture is not publicly disclosed in contract terms, but Nirav Prajapati has described its structure directly in a published interview: "We started thinking revenue would be driven primarily by data pulls. Today, our revenue is increasingly outcome-linked and intelligence-led. We have usage-based pricing on orchestration and data access, premium pricing for intelligence modules covering income, risk, personas, and collections signals, and enterprise contracts tied to scale, reliability, and business impact. The shift has been from pay for access to pay for outcomes."[14]

This three-tier architecture is standard for mature API-led fintech infrastructure companies. The entry tier is usage-based pricing on AA orchestration, which is how most of the 150-plus institutions likely first engage the platform. The intelligence tier covers Income Analysis, MSME Underwriting, Customer Data Intelligence, and collections signals at a premium above base orchestration. The enterprise tier covers institutions at the scale of L&T Finance, structured around annual committed volumes, SLA guarantees, and business impact clauses. The AI Collections module, launched in February 2026, introduces Voice AI agent pricing at an outcome level: cost per resolved collection conversation or per successful mandate representation, consistent with industry convention for agentic AI products in collections workflows.[51]

No gross margin data has been publicly disclosed. The structural benchmark for B2B SaaS companies delivering primarily software and intelligence rather than raw compute is 70 to 80 percent gross margin at scale.[52] Ignosis's local deployment option means inference compute costs on the intelligence modules can be borne by the customer, which is a meaningful margin advantage over cloud-native AI products. The AI Collections Voice AI module will compress margins on that tier in the near term as conversation volume scales to amortise fixed model costs, consistent with industry-wide dynamics for agentic AI products in early deployment.

The company reported profitability in FY2025 on INR 10.6 crore in revenue with approximately 35 to 37 employees, implying the INR 35.5 crore from the pre-Series A is being deployed to fund growth investment, specifically engineering expansion, commercial hires, and AI product development, rather than to cover operating losses. This is a structurally healthier deployment of capital than a company using its latest round to fund a negative unit economics deficit.

Net Revenue Retention is the metric most consequential for the Series A case and the one this memo cannot assess from public data. A platform whose institutional customers are expanding from AA orchestration to income analysis to collections intelligence, as described in the Kissht case study, should demonstrate NRR above 120 percent at the cohort level for accounts active more than twelve months.[52] If that pattern holds across the broader institutional base, the revenue model is compounding. Confirming this number is the foundational diligence task.

The company's stated use of pre-Series A capital covers four priorities: scaling engineering, business, and compliance teams; expanding the AI product roadmap including finance-specific LLMs and agentic AI use cases for BFSI; deepening governance and compliance efforts; and converting the installed base of 150-plus institutions from single-module to multi-module enterprise accounts.[4][35] The last of these is the highest-value capital deployment available: the average revenue per institution at FY2025 close implies most customers are on the base orchestration tier only, and the commercial investment required to expand those relationships toward the intelligence and collections modules is the mechanism through which the Series A revenue target is built.

Part 09

Why Now and Thesis Fit

Three forces are converging simultaneously and with unusual precision. Their convergence is not coincidental. It is the investment thesis.

Force 1: The AA ecosystem has crossed the adoption threshold that creates urgency, not optionality.

In December 2023, 39 million accounts were linked on the AA framework. By December 2024, that figure had grown to 120 million. By September 2025, 2.2 billion financial accounts were enabled for consent-based data sharing and USD 10 billion-plus in loans had been disbursed through the ecosystem, with half of that total disbursed in the six months between April and September 2024 alone.[25][30] NBFCs, which drove 60 percent of all completed AA consents in FY2025, are now procuring AA infrastructure not as an experiment but as a baseline operational requirement for competitive lending.[26] This shift changes the nature of Ignosis's sales conversation completely. A financial institution evaluating Ignosis in 2023 was being asked to adopt an emerging technology. A financial institution evaluating Ignosis in 2026 is being asked to solve a live operational bottleneck.

Force 2: The DPDP Act compliance clock is compressing the window for institutions still using non-compliant data collection architectures.

The Digital Personal Data Protection Rules 2025, notified on November 13, 2025, create a phased enforcement timeline with hard deadlines: Consent Manager registration opens November 2026, full substantive obligations become enforceable from May 13, 2027, with penalties up to INR 250 crore per contravention.[32][54] For financial institutions relying on PDF parsing, screen scraping, or bulk data collection, this compliance clock is a forcing function. Ignosis's AA-native architecture is structurally compliant by construction: all data is fetched through user-consented, encrypted, time-limited, purpose-specific consent requests. Sahamati has publicly argued that operating Account Aggregators should be deemed Consent Managers for the financial sector under DPDP without requiring a separate registration.[43] If this regulatory outcome is accepted, every institution integrated with the AA ecosystem through Ignosis would inherit DPDP Consent Manager compliance automatically. This is a competitive advantage not yet priced into the market.

Force 3: The AI inflection in Indian BFSI collections and underwriting has reached the product categories Ignosis launched into in early 2026.

The first wave of AI adoption in Indian BFSI, covering chatbots and customer support automation, is a competed market. The second wave, covering underwriting intelligence, risk scoring, and collections automation, is beginning to generate the verifiable institutional outcomes that drive procurement decisions.[33] Ignosis's AI Collections module launched at precisely the moment when the market is beginning to pay for verified outcomes. India's total system credit outstanding stands at INR 189 lakh crore.[28] Collections infrastructure for even a marginal improvement in recovery rates across this base represents enormous addressable spend. The combination of AA-native data as the signal layer, machine learning models trained on 50 million-plus user profiles as the intelligence layer, and Voice AI agents as the execution layer is a product architecture that could not have existed in 2022. The AA infrastructure was immature. The behavioural dataset was too small. The Voice AI capability was not production-grade for complex financial conversations. All three constraints have resolved in the period Ignosis has been operating. The founding date was correct. The product launch dates are correctly timed.

Thesis Fit

Sector fit India Stack native B2B infrastructure with a proprietary data intelligence layer and expanding agentic AI capabilities. Enterprise sales motion with a defined institutional buyer. Revenue is recurring and expands as both AA adoption and the installed customer base grow. This is infrastructure, not consumer product, and it is the category that 45 percent of private Indian fintech funding in 2024 went to.[23]
Stage fit Pre-Series A. The company has live revenue at INR 10.6 crore with 154 percent annual growth, confirmed profitability, 150-plus named institutional customers, and three publicly referenceable production deployments. The commercial risk is the speed and attach rate of the AI Collections module, not the existence of the core business.
Geography fit India beachhead with no defined international expansion plan announced, which is appropriate at this stage. The India BFSI market is large enough for a strong Series A and Series B trajectory before Southeast Asia or Middle East expansion is relevant.
Check size fit A Series A participation of INR 15 crore to INR 25 crore targeting 5 to 8 percent ownership is the appropriate entry point, contingent on confirmed FY2026 ARR and NRR data. The Series A conversation should be initiated once FY2026 H1 revenue is confirmed and at least three existing customers have adopted the AI Collections module on a paid contract.
Part 10

Key Risks and Open Questions

Key Risks

Risk 1: The FY2026 ARR trajectory after the AI Collections launch is unknown. Severity: High until confirmed

The INR 10.6 crore FY2025 revenue figure is the most important anchor in this memo and it is now six months old. The AI Collections module launched in February 2026, adding Voice AI agents, Smart Mandate optimisation, and Case Allocation. The critical unknown is whether the launch has already generated paid commercial contracts or whether it is still in the institutional evaluation phase. The FY2026 contracted ARR and the pipeline conversion rate for AI Collections must be the first numbers requested in any founder engagement. The Series A conversation should not proceed until both figures are confirmed.

Risk 2: Average revenue per institution implies most customers are on the lowest-value product tier. Severity: Medium to High until NRR confirmed

INR 10.6 crore across 150-plus institutions implies average revenue per institution of approximately INR 7 lakh per year. This is low relative to comparable Indian B2B infrastructure companies charging INR 20 lakh to INR 50 lakh per year for equivalent-scope platform contracts. The most consequential commercial question this memo cannot answer is the Net Revenue Retention for cohorts active for more than twelve months, alongside the module attach rate across the installed base and the average contract value for the top 20 institutional customers by revenue. NRR above 120 percent converts this from a customer-acquisition-led growth story into a compounding platform story.

Risk 3: Perfios is IPO-preparing, well-capitalised, and actively building AI capabilities on an AA-integrated platform. Severity: High and persistent

Perfios has INR 3,225 crore in funding, INR 709 crore in FY2025 revenue, 1,042 employees, and is widely considered IPO-ready.[39][57] It operates Anumati, a licensed NBFC-AA, and launched a GenAI platform for banking in July 2025 and an AI operating system for BFSI in December 2025. The specific competitive scenario that warrants monitoring is Perfios extending its AA-native intelligence products directly into the income verification, MSME underwriting, and collections signal use cases that Ignosis currently leads. Perfios has the data asset, the engineering capacity, and the existing financial institution relationships to move there without requiring new customer acquisition. The mitigant is switching cost: an institution that has calibrated its credit underwriting models on Ignosis's specific income imputation outputs cannot substitute Perfios's outputs without recalibrating the entire model stack. That switching cost compounds with every passing quarter of production usage. The Series A thesis must be that Ignosis builds this switching cost depth faster than Perfios closes the product gap.

Risk 4: The AA-native model is dependent on AA ecosystem health outside Ignosis's control. Severity: Low to Medium

Ignosis's entire product architecture is built on the AA framework. A regulatory reversal, systemic trust failure, or period of significant technical degradation across licensed AAs would directly affect Ignosis in ways that a more diversified data infrastructure company would not be. This risk is low in probability given government backing at the highest level, 17 licensed operators, and clear regulatory momentum. The more realistic manifestation is a period of elevated AA failure rates driven by one or two licensed AAs experiencing technical issues, reducing the end-to-end success rate that Ignosis has built its value proposition around. The multi-AA routing architecture directly mitigates this: Ignosis's router can deprioritise a failing AA provider in real time and route to alternatives, as demonstrated in the L&T Finance deployment.[36]

Risk 5: The NBFC-AA subsidiary status is unconfirmed and the Pirimid Fintech relationship requires documentation. Severity: Low if resolved, Medium if stalled

Ignosi Account Aggregator Private Limited was incorporated in May 2024 and is registered under the Other Financial Activities classification.[2] If a NBFC-AA licence application has been filed, the approval process typically takes 12 to 18 months. Whether the application has been filed, its current stage, and the company's strategic intent for the subsidiary must be confirmed in diligence. A confirmed licence would allow Ignosis to own the full data relay chain from financial institution to lender, a meaningful structural upgrade to the competitive position. Separately, Nirav Prajapati is simultaneously the founder of Pirimid Fintech, which was acquired 49 percent by Infibeam Avenues in December 2023.[12] The degree to which his time and attention is split between the two companies, and whether any intellectual property or client relationships overlap, must be confirmed. This is not a red flag. It is a structural question that requires a clear answer before any investment decision proceeds.

Question 2 What is the Net Revenue Retention for cohorts active for more than twelve months? Has any existing customer on the base AA orchestration tier contracted the AI Collections module on a paid basis since the February 2026 launch?

Question 5 What is Chintan Sheth's specific role in the AI and machine learning model development for the income verification and collections intelligence products? Are there additional senior ML engineers on the team beyond the founding technical staff?

Rationale

Ignosis is not a pass. The company has built the deepest AA orchestration capability available from a non-bank Technology Service Provider in India, has done so from the first operational day of the AA framework, and has accumulated four years of proprietary financial intelligence data from 150-plus live institutional deployments. The revenue profile is unusual and positive for this stage: INR 10.6 crore in FY2025 at a 154 percent year-on-year CAGR, combined with reported profitability, on INR 46.2 crore in total capital raised. The L&T Finance and Kissht case studies provide third-party validation of production-grade performance at the scale of the institutions this market cares about. The founder's governance position inside the Sahamati TSP steering committee is a structural information advantage that no competitor can acquire quickly. The DPDP compliance clock and the AA adoption inflection are creating procurement urgency among the institutions not yet on the platform.

However, this memo cannot answer the four questions on which the entire investment case turns: the FY2026 contracted ARR following the AI Collections launch, the Net Revenue Retention for cohorts active more than twelve months, the status of the NBFC-AA licence application for the regulated subsidiary, and the degree to which AI Collections has converted from product launch to commercial contract in the four months since February 2026. These are not peripheral diligence items. They are the load-bearing facts under the valuation, the commercial trajectory, and the product expansion strategy simultaneously. Placing capital before those questions are answered means pricing a Series A investment on pre-Series A information at a moment when the company is executing a major product expansion. That is not the right entry point. The correct posture is active monitoring with a defined set of conditions that, if met, trigger a Series A conversation on a short timeline.

Conditions for Re-engagement at Series A

Condition 1: FY2026 ARR confirmed and AI Collections adoption demonstrated. A revenue figure of INR 20 crore or above, with evidence that at least 20 to 25 percent is attributable to the intelligence and collections modules rather than the base AA orchestration tier, is the minimum threshold for a Series A conversation consistent with the 8 to 12 times ARR range applicable to Indian BFSI SaaS infrastructure companies with this growth profile. The module-level revenue breakdown must be disclosed.

Condition 2: Net Revenue Retention above 120 percent confirmed for cohorts active more than twelve months. NRR above 120 percent is the single metric that converts this business from a customer-acquisition-led growth story into a compounding platform story. If NRR is below 110 percent, the platform is growing from new institutional acquisition rather than within-account expansion and the Series A narrative requires fundamental revision. This metric must be documented, not claimed.

Condition 3: The NBFC-AA subsidiary status and Pirimid Fintech relationship fully documented. The status of any NBFC-AA licence application must be confirmed and documented. Separately, the nature of Nirav Prajapati's ongoing involvement at Pirimid Fintech, any IP that originated at Pirimid and is now embedded in the Ignosis platform, and any shared client relationships must be fully disclosed with supporting evidence. An incomplete or vague answer to either question must be resolved before Series A capital is committed.

Condition 4: At least one named enterprise customer willing to take a public reference call for the AI Collections module. The AI Collections module was launched in February 2026. By the time of any Series A investor diligence in the second half of 2026, the module will have been in the market for six to nine months. A module that has not produced a single named, referenceable enterprise customer in that period raises a question about whether the product has cleared the procurement threshold for the buyer profile it is targeting.

Key Reasons to Invest at Series A if Conditions are Met

The first reason is the founder's structural position inside the AA ecosystem. Nirav Prajapati was a registered TSP in the AA ecosystem from its first operational day, built the technical AA integrations that Ignosis was founded on, and now serves as Joint-Convenor of Sahamati's Associate Member TSP Steering Committee.[14] That role provides regulatory early visibility, ecosystem governance influence, and institutional relationships with the RBI and Sahamati that no competitor can manufacture retrospectively. In a market where the regulatory infrastructure is still developing and compliance rules are still being written, a founder inside the governance structure of that infrastructure has a compounding advantage that is unique in the Indian fintech intelligence category.

The second reason is the data flywheel depth. The 50 million-plus user financial profiles accumulated over four years of live production deployments represent a training corpus that is genuinely difficult for a competitor to replicate from a standing start.[8] The specific value is not the size but the composition: cashflow patterns, income volatility signatures, and credit outcome histories of thin-file borrowers, gig workers, and MSME proprietors in India, the profiles that matter most for the next wave of credit expansion and which are structurally underrepresented in any dataset built primarily on PDF document parsing. Perfios has a larger overall dataset but one built on a different data collection model. The AA-native signal set is not a subset of what Perfios holds. It is a qualitatively different asset.

The third reason is that the regulatory tailwind is widening with every compliance quarter. The DPDP Act enforcement timeline makes Ignosis's consent-based, AA-native architecture progressively more valuable relative to competitors relying on document parsing, bulk data collection, or screen scraping. Sahamati's argument that operating AAs should be deemed Consent Managers for financial data without a separate registration, if accepted by the Data Protection Board, would convert Ignosis's institutional customers into DPDP-compliant data fiduciaries automatically.[43] If that regulatory outcome is confirmed, it shortens the compliance sales cycle at precisely the moment when DPDP enforcement urgency is highest.

Key Risks Accepted at Series A

The Perfios competitive risk is accepted as a known, actively monitored risk. Perfios is IPO-preparing, has INR 3,225 crore in funding, and is building AI capabilities on an AA-integrated platform.[39][57] The Series A investment thesis is that Ignosis builds switching-cost depth, through the recalibration of underwriting models around its specific income imputation outputs and the embedding of its collections AI into the daily operational workflows of its institutional customer base, faster than Perfios closes the product gap. That race is active and must be acknowledged explicitly, not assumed away.

The average revenue per institution risk is accepted on the condition that Condition 1 is satisfied and the module-level ARR breakdown has been disclosed. If the AI Collections attach rate is growing, the INR 7 lakh average revenue per institution at FY2025 close is a launching pad, not a ceiling. If the attach rate is static, the valuation conversation requires revision. The founder time-allocation risk associated with Pirimid Fintech is accepted conditionally, on the explicit basis that Condition 3 has been fully resolved with documentation. An unresolved IP or time-allocation ambiguity at Series A is a legal and governance risk that institutional investors will identify in due diligence. It must be clean before the first term sheet is signed.
