The Quiet Infrastructure Play Hiding Inside India's AI Boom

Synthetic data isn't a workaround. In India, it's becoming the foundation.

By Arjun Chatterjee

I'll admit upfront that I'm not a neutral observer here. During my PhD, I used synthetic data to approximate solutions to options pricing PDEs, specifically in regimes where real market data was too sparse, too noisy, or simply unavailable for the boundary conditions I needed. It worked. Not as a shortcut, but as a genuinely rigorous alternative. That experience left me with a lasting bias: synthetic data is not what you use when you can't get the real thing. It is, in the right context, the better thing.

India in 2025 is shaping up to be exactly that context, at a scale that should interest every serious AI investor.

Every serious AI investor knows the playbook by now: find a sector ripe for disruption, back a team that can build a model, and wait for the moat to form around proprietary data. It's a clean thesis. It also has a structural problem that most pitch decks politely skip over.

Building training datasets in India is brutally slow, legally precarious, and increasingly expensive. Consider the healthcare startup that needs 500,000 annotated radiology images to prove clinical accuracy, or the fintech building a fraud detection engine that can't touch live transaction records during development, or the multilingual AI company trying to train on dialects that don't exist in any corpus. These aren't edge cases. They are the dominant operating reality for the majority of AI startups across India's most valuable verticals. And the problem is about to get significantly worse.

This is the gap that a new category of AI infrastructure startup is positioning to fill: synthetic data generation purpose-built for Indian market conditions. The opportunity is real, the timing is precise, and the category is still largely unclaimed.

The Regulatory Gun That Just Went Off

On November 13, 2025, the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology notified India's long-awaited Digital Personal Data Protection (DPDP) Rules, formally operationalizing the DPDP Act of 2023. With this, roughly 850 million internet users, approximately 15% of the world's digital population, came under the ambit of a comprehensive privacy framework for the first time.

The implications for AI companies are not subtle. Under the Act's framework, any startup that determines the purpose and manner of processing personal data is classified as a Data Fiduciary and bears full legal accountability, even when using third-party annotation vendors or cloud providers. AI systems that influence significant decisions in credit, hiring, or health are subject to substantially elevated transparency and fairness obligations. Training AI on personal data now requires explicit consent and a clear stated purpose. The long-standing assumption that publicly scraped data is freely usable no longer holds.

The compliance clock is running. Full substantive obligations, covering privacy notices, consent systems, breach protocols, data retention, and rights management, become enforceable by May 13, 2027. Non-compliance carries penalties of up to ₹250 crore per violation. For context, IBM's most recent data on India estimates the average cost of a breach at approximately ₹220 million, driven by incident response, downtime, and customer trust erosion. That's before regulatory penalties are applied.

Synthetic data transitions from a technical preference to a compliance strategy. This is what regulatory moats look like before the market prices them in.

For AI startups, the calculus is straightforward: continuing to train on real personal data is now a liability event waiting to happen. Synthetic data, which is privacy-preserving by construction and audit-friendly by design, transitions from a technical preference to a compliance strategy. Deloitte India has already published compliance blueprints framing synthetic data and Privacy-by-Design as core mechanisms for meeting DPDP obligations. Ernst & Young's Indian practice has similarly flagged data minimization and synthetic alternatives as primary pathways for AI companies navigating the new rules.

A Market That Rewards Early Infrastructure Bets

The global synthetic data generation market stood at $351 million in 2023 and is projected by Fortune Business Insights to reach $2.3 billion by 2030, growing at a CAGR of 31%. India-specific projections are steeper: from $25 million in 2024 to over $2 billion by 2035, a CAGR approaching 49% and one of the fastest growth trajectories in any AI sub-segment globally.

Gartner's framing of the category is important for any investor constructing a thesis here. In its 2023 data science trends research, Gartner predicted that 60% of data used to develop AI and analytics projects would be synthetically generated, up from 1% in 2021. Its 2025 Hype Cycle for Artificial Intelligence flags synthetic data as gaining meaningful traction specifically in healthcare and finance, precisely the sectors where real-world data is hardest to access.

The investment signal from global markets is already clear. In 2022, MOSTLY AI raised a $25 million Series B led by Molten Ventures, Datagen landed $50 million led by Scale Venture Partners, and Synthesis AI closed a $17 million Series A. These rounds reflect a conviction, shared by Quiet Capital's Astasia Myers among others, that synthetic data tooling will become as essential as cloud infrastructure. Every AI company will eventually need it. India has yet to produce a scaled, defensible player in this category.

Three Verticals Where India's Data Problem Is Acute

Healthcare

India faces a severe shortage of specialist physicians, with a doctor-to-patient ratio that remains well below WHO recommendations, which is why AI diagnostics have attracted significant venture attention. Startups working in radiology, pathology, and oncology screening need enormous volumes of annotated imaging data to demonstrate clinical-grade accuracy. But as research published in Radiology makes explicit, data scarcity is the binding constraint on this entire category: rare conditions are underrepresented, demographic biases are pervasive, and privacy regulations make sharing patient data across institutions nearly impossible.

Synthetic medical imaging, generated via diffusion models and GANs, has moved from academic curiosity to practical tool. For an Indian startup building AI for rural or Tier-2 diagnostics, where patient demographic profiles diverge sharply from Western training sets, the ability to generate India-specific synthetic imaging data is not incremental. It is foundational.

Fintech

India's fintech sector was valued at $584 billion in 2022, with estimates projecting $1.5 trillion by 2025. Fraud detection, credit scoring, and risk modeling all depend on access to historical transaction data that is, under the DPDP framework, firmly in the category of sensitive personal data. Real-time transaction records cannot be shared with third-party vendors, cannot be used freely in development environments, and must be deleted once processing purposes are fulfilled. Synthetic tabular data, which preserves the statistical properties of real financial data without exposing any individual record, is the only workable solution for developing and testing these models at scale within the new regulatory constraints.

This is the use case closest to my own experience. When I was working on options pricing PDEs, the core challenge wasn't computational power, it was that real derivatives data was thin at the tails of the distribution, exactly where pricing accuracy matters most. Synthetic data let me populate those regimes with statistically faithful samples and validate model behaviour under stress conditions I couldn't observe in historical records. Fintech AI in India faces the same structural problem, just at enterprise scale and with a regulatory layer added on top.

Multilingual AI

India has 22 constitutionally recognized languages and over 1,500 recorded dialects. The IndiaAI Mission's own foundation model ambitions, including the government-backed BharatGen multimodal LLM, are predicated on access to large, high-quality, India-specific language datasets. Such datasets do not exist in meaningful volume for most Indian languages. Synthetic text and speech generation tailored to Indian linguistic contexts is an infrastructure gap that a US or European synthetic data company has neither the incentive nor the cultural context to fill. This is white space with a government mandate behind it.

The Government as Structural Co-investor

The IndiaAI Mission, approved by the Union Cabinet in March 2024 with a ₹10,372 crore outlay, is the most significant state-led AI infrastructure commitment in India's history. The mission has deployed over 38,000 GPUs, with high-end compute available to startups at a subsidized rate of ₹65 per hour, a deliberate effort to de-risk the capital formation phase for early-stage AI companies.

This matters for synthetic data startups for a specific reason: generating high-quality synthetic data at scale, particularly for imaging and multimodal applications, is computationally intensive. Access to affordable GPU infrastructure through the IndiaAI compute pillar removes what would otherwise be a significant barrier to experimentation and early product development. Stanford University's 2025 Global AI Vibrancy Tool ranked India third globally in AI competitiveness, a ranking that reflects not just startup activity but the depth of government-backed infrastructure now available to founders.

What the Defensible Startup Looks Like

The market map for synthetic data in India currently has no dominant player. Global tools from MOSTLY AI, Gretel, and Synthetic Data Vault are generic by design, built for Western regulatory contexts and without the domain-specific customization or multilingual capability that Indian verticals demand. The opening is for a company that is India-first by design, not India-adapted after the fact.

The winning architecture will likely have three components. First, domain-specific generation engines: not a general-purpose synthetic data tool, but purpose-built modules for healthcare imaging, fintech tabular data, and Indian-language text and speech. Second, DPDP-native governance covering audit trails, consent lineage tracking, and documentation designed specifically to satisfy India's new compliance requirements, not retrofitted from GDPR frameworks. Third, an enterprise distribution motion targeting the 1,800+ Global Capability Centers operating in India, the large fintech and health-tech companies navigating DPDP, and the growing base of AI-first startups that can't afford the 18-month dataset-building cycles of the previous generation.

The companies that built storage and compute tooling in 2007 did not make the headlines. They made the returns.

I came to trust synthetic data through a fairly narrow numerical problem in a PhD program. What I find compelling about India's current moment is that the same underlying logic applies across an entire economy's AI stack: faithfully generated data can substitute for, and sometimes outperform, real data in constrained regimes. The infrastructure bet here is not exotic. It is, if anything, overdue.

In India's AI moment, synthetic data is the unsexy infrastructure play that underpins the exciting application layer. The parallel in cloud computing is instructive: the companies that built storage and compute tooling in 2007 did not make the headlines. They made the returns.
