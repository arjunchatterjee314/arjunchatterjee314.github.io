The Application Layer Bet

Indian VCs are deliberately not trying to build the next OpenAI. Understanding why that is a strategy, not a concession, is the most important framing question in Indian AI right now.

By Arjun Chatterjee

In December 2025, Prayank Swaroop, a partner at Accel India, told TechCrunch something that deserved more attention than it received in the ecosystem's year-end retrospectives. "We don't yet have an AI-first company in India which is $40–50 million of revenue, if not $100 million, in a year's time frame," he said, "and that is globally happening." He went on: India lacks large foundational model companies and will take time to build the research depth, talent pipeline, and patient capital needed to compete at that layer. Application-led AI and adjacent deep-tech, he concluded, is the more realistic focus for the near term.

This was not a lament. It was a thesis. And it is the thesis that now governs where serious Indian VC capital is going.

The question worth asking — and the one this piece tries to answer — is whether that thesis is a prudent adaptation to India's structural realities, or a strategic miscalculation that will leave the country permanently on the consuming end of the most valuable technology transition in a generation.

The Numbers Behind the Choice

Start with the capital disparity, because it is the clearest articulation of the strategic fork in the road.

Per Tracxn data published in late December 2025, India's AI startups raised $643 million across 100 deals across all of 2025. In the same period, US AI funding surged past $121 billion across 765 rounds, a 141% jump from 2024. Crunchbase's end-of-year analysis sharpens the picture further: foundation model companies alone raised $80 billion in 2025, representing 40% of all global AI funding. OpenAI and Anthropic between them captured 14% of all global venture investment for the year.

India's share of global AI venture funding: roughly 0.5%.

That gap is not a market-timing artifact. It reflects a deliberate set of choices made by the most active and credible investors in the Indian ecosystem. Lightspeed India managing director Bejul Somaia articulated the logic directly: Lightspeed had just raised $9 billion in fresh capital globally with a strong focus on AI, but the India arm would continue backing consumer startups alongside selectively exploring AI opportunities shaped by local demand rather than global capital intensity.

The phrase "local demand rather than global capital intensity" is doing significant work in that sentence. It is a precise description of the application layer bet.

What the Application Layer Bet Actually Means

The distinction between foundation model investment and application layer investment is not simply about check size. It is about where in the AI value chain a company stakes its claim to defensibility.

Foundation model companies — OpenAI, Anthropic, Mistral, Cohere, and their peers — bet that the model itself is the moat. Training a frontier-scale model requires hundreds of millions to billions of dollars in compute, enormous proprietary datasets, and research teams capable of pushing the architectural frontier. The companies that succeed at this layer own the infrastructure of the AI economy.

Application layer companies make a different claim: that the model is a commodity input, and the moat lies in workflow integration, distribution, proprietary data generated through usage, and domain depth. The most successful examples globally — Harvey in legal AI, Cursor in developer tooling, Glean in enterprise knowledge retrieval — are all applications built on top of OpenAI or Anthropic APIs, not foundational research efforts. Their defensibility does not come from the model. It comes from the customer relationship, the feedback loop, and the vertical specificity.

India's VC consensus is that this second path is the right one for Indian founders right now, grounded in three structural realities specific to India's market conditions.

The first is the R&D infrastructure gap. According to WIPO's Global Innovation Index 2025, India's gross expenditure on R&D is approximately 0.64% of GDP — well below the global average and a fraction of the 3.5% spent by the United States and 2.65% by China. Critically, India's private sector accounts for only 36–37% of total R&D spending, compared to 68% in the US and 75% in China. Foundation model development is a private-sector R&D activity. Without a private sector that treats deep research as a competitive input, the infrastructure for training frontier models simply does not exist at the required scale.

The second is compute access. I say this with some personal embarrassment: years ago, before any of this was a venture capital conversation, I sat in my bedroom and tried to train a model myself. I had a decent GPU, reasonable conviction, and almost no understanding of what frontier training actually costs. The exercise lasted about two days before I hit a wall that no enthusiasm could climb over. What I was attempting naively, with consumer hardware and a university internet connection, the serious players were doing with data centres consuming hundreds of megawatts and budgets measured in nine figures. Training a competitive large language model at frontier scale requires sustained access to GPU clusters at a cost and duration that no Indian startup has yet been able to finance without government support. The IndiaAI Mission's ₹10,372 crore deployment of 38,000 GPUs represents the most significant intervention to address this gap — but it is a subsidy architecture, not a market architecture.

The third is the talent pipeline. World-class AI research requires a concentration of researchers who have trained at the frontier and who choose to remain in or return to India. That pipeline is improving — IIT and IISc outputs are globally competitive, and the Indian diaspora in US AI labs is substantial. But the domestic retention rate is not yet at the level required to staff a frontier model lab.

Krutrim and the Cost of Ignoring These Constraints

If the structural argument for the application layer bet needed a case study, Krutrim provided it across 2025.

In January 2024, Ola founder Bhavish Aggarwal announced that Krutrim had raised $50 million from Z47 Partners at a $1 billion valuation, making it India's first AI unicorn and the fastest Indian startup ever to reach that milestone. The pitch was foundational and maximalist: chips to chatbots, entirely within India, for India. Aggarwal personally committed $230 million. The narrative was exactly what India's tech ecosystem wanted to hear.

By the time Outlook Business published its cover investigation on January 25, 2026, the picture was considerably more sobering. Krutrim had undergone three rounds of layoffs through 2025, primarily affecting its linguistics team — the group most essential to its multilingual AI differentiation — with close to 200 employees let go in total. At least six senior executives had departed since April, including leaders in AI data, cloud architecture, and software engineering. The fundraise target had been quietly cut from $500 million to $300 million amid tepid investor interest.

Kruti, the consumer AI assistant app launched in mid-2025 as Krutrim's flagship product, had accumulated approximately 100,000 downloads on Google Play by December. Revenues for FY25, reported at ₹101.7 crore, came 90% from Ola group entities, raising pointed questions about whether Krutrim had any market beyond its founder's own companies.

The Krutrim story is not about personal failure. It is about the cost of attempting to build a full-stack AI foundational company in a market where the structural conditions for doing so do not yet exist.

Business Standard noted that unlike high-profile AI founders in the West, Aggarwal had not yet established credibility in the AI domain specifically, leaving investors unconvinced about the need for large capital injections into a company still in early execution with no peer-reviewed research to its name. That observation carries a lesson that extends well beyond Krutrim: in foundation model development, technical credibility is not a soft factor. It is the primary underwriting criterion.

Where the Application Layer Bet Is Playing Out

The companies that best represent the application layer thesis in India are not trying to out-train OpenAI. They are trying to out-distribute it in verticals where Indian context creates a genuine competitive advantage.

Sarvam AI is the most instructive example precisely because it occupies a middle ground. Founded in 2023 by Vivek Raghavan and Pratyush Kumar — both of whom previously worked at AI4Bharat at IIT Madras — Sarvam raised $41 million in a Series A led by Lightspeed with participation from Peak XV and Khosla Ventures in December 2023, the largest Series A for an Indian AI startup at the time. The pitch was not to build a ChatGPT competitor. It was to build population-scale AI for India's linguistic and infrastructural reality: voice-first interfaces, 22 constitutionally recognized languages, and deployment economics suited to a market where most users are on low-end devices with intermittent connectivity.

In April 2025, the government selected Sarvam under the IndiaAI Mission to develop India's sovereign large language model, providing both mandate and compute access. Sarvam has released Sarvam-1, a 2-billion-parameter model optimized for Indian languages and voice interfaces, and has positioned itself as infrastructure for Indian enterprise and government AI deployment rather than a consumer chatbot competing directly with OpenAI.

The Sarvam model, from a VC perspective, is coherent: use government-backed compute to develop foundational Indian-language capability, then monetize through an enterprise and government distribution motion that global players are structurally unable to compete with. The application layer thesis, applied at the India-specific infrastructure layer.

The Question the Thesis Cannot Avoid

The application layer bet has an internal tension that sophisticated investors are beginning to surface explicitly.

If the models that power application layer companies are owned by OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google, and those models continue to improve rapidly, then the moats being built at the application layer are only as durable as the rate of model improvement allows. A customer relationship built on top of GPT-4 can be disrupted by GPT-5 if GPT-5 natively handles the vertical use case that the application company spent two years building. Sequoia's Sonya Huang flagged this directly at AI Ascent 2025: application layer companies face margin compression from inference costs, competition from foundation model feature expansion, and uncertainty about defensibility as models improve.

India's VC community is aware of this tension. The implicit answer embedded in most application layer investment theses is that Indian-context data — transaction patterns, language diversity, healthcare imaging characteristics, agricultural sensor readings — creates a proprietary data moat that global foundation models cannot easily replicate. The startup that generates millions of Indian-language voice interactions through a product, or that underwrites credit for millions of thin-file Indian borrowers, is accumulating data that no amount of US compute spending can substitute for.

Whether that moat is durable enough to compound over a ten-year investment horizon is the open question. The answer will determine whether the application layer bet is remembered as a shrewd capital efficiency play or as the strategic concession that locked India out of the highest-value layer of the AI economy.

What is not in question is that Indian VCs have made a choice. It is a more considered choice than it is often given credit for, grounded in real structural constraints and real capital efficiency logic. The case for it is strong. The case against it deserves to be heard as well. And the companies building at that application layer — with India-specific data moats and distribution advantages that no San Francisco startup can replicate — are the ones worth watching most closely as the answer becomes clear.
